Question 1

Market Understanding

Trevel sells to three very different people. I've mapped who they are, what actually triggers a booking, and where each one could slip away — then picked the one this plan is built for.

Daily Commuters

Executive tier · ₹8,999/mo

Who

Working professionals, 26–42, at Gurugram corporate hubs — Cyberhub, Golf Course Road, Udyog Vihar. Could afford a car, don't want the daily traffic and parking stress of driving it.

Decision trigger

A bad commute morning — a cancelled Ola/Uber 10 minutes before a meeting, or 3x surge in the rain. The switch is reactive, not proactive.

Why not Trevel

The subscription needs upfront commitment. Hybrid/WFH schedules may not generate enough rides to justify the fixed cost.

Competitor move

Uber Reserve's scheduled rides chip at the reliability pitch. Response: reliability is structural here (owned fleet, salaried drivers) — not a feature bolted onto a marketplace.

Corporate Clients

B2B employee transport

Who

HR/Admin/Facilities heads at 200–2,000-employee companies, buying on an annual procurement cycle — not a daily decision.

Decision trigger

A renewal cycle with an incumbent vendor, usually paired with an incident — an accident, a safety audit, or escalated employee complaints.

Why not Trevel

Multi-year lock-ins and sunk capex with existing fleet vendors. The blocker is switching cost, not product quality.

Competitor move

Fleet vendors are pitching EVs for ESG targets. Response: Trevel already owns the EV fleet — lead with ready-made emissions reporting they can't match yet.

Airport Transfers

One-off, high-intent

Who

Frequent flyers, 28–50, booking a one-off trip to/from IGI — outside their normal commute pattern entirely.

Decision trigger

The night before a flight, when a missed pickup means a missed flight — stakes far higher than a late office arrival.

Why not Trevel

Zero loyalty pull. It's a price/speed comparison in the moment — Trevel has to win the booking, not the relationship.

Competitor move

Uber/Ola's "guaranteed pickup" products close this gap. Response: match the guarantee, but make it provable — an on-time-or-refund policy, backed by salaried drivers with no cancel-for-surge incentive.

Why Daily Commuters, for Q2–Q3

Every baseline metric Trevel gave — installs, active riders, repeat share — is a recurring-usage metric, not a one-off booking metric. That's the Commuter segment. It's also the fastest-moving segment for a 1–3 person team: no long B2B sales cycle like Corporate, and a growth engine built on habit rather than one-time bookings like Airport.

Question 2

Marketing Strategy

A one-page plan for the Commuter segment — built to fix the conversion leak, not just feed the top of the funnel.

Objective

Grow new Executive-tier sign-ups from ~200 to 240 new riders/month (+20%) in 30 days, holding blended CAC at or below ₹500 — by improving the install→first-ride conversion rate, not just buying more installs.

Buying more top-of-funnel installs without fixing the 80% who never ride just inflates spend without inflating riders.

Target Audience

28–40, Gurugram corporate micro-markets, commuting 5 days/week to a fixed office, ₹15–40L household income, currently on Ola/Uber/own car — and has had at least one recent bad commute. That last trait is the real targeting signal.

Positioning

"For Gurugram commuters tired of gambling on their ride to work, Trevel is the subscription EV service that guarantees the same car, same driver, same price — every day — because it owns its fleet instead of renting someone else's unpredictability."

Acquisition Mix

  • Geo-targeted paid social/search near named corporate parks — intent concentrates here, city-wide targeting wastes spend.
  • WhatsApp referral — cheapest CAC, and matches infra Trevel already has.
  • On-ground QR/OOH at metro stops & park gates — the decision trigger happens right there.
  • Deliberately skipping broad TV/print and generic search — priced for Ola/Uber's budget scale, not a ₹1L/month team.

Retention Strategy

  • Repeat riders already drive 70% of bookings — protect that base first.
  • WhatsApp renewal nudges 5 days before subscription expiry.
  • A ride "streak" mechanic — small credit after 20 rides in a cycle.
  • Usage-triggered tier-upgrade nudges (Commuter → Executive) — upsell beats new acquisition on cost.

Question 4

The Growth Challenge

+₹1,00,000, 30 days, 50% growth on installs and bookings — without letting CAC drift above ₹500.

1,000 installs × 20% install→first-ride = 200 new riders. ₹1,00,000 ÷ 200 = ₹500 CAC. That's where the number comes from — a cost per new rider, not per install.

Grow installs 50% to 1,500 at the same 20% conversion → 300 new riders. Spend the full ₹2,00,000 against that, and CAC becomes:

₹2,00,000 ÷ 300 = ₹667 CAC — 33% over target.

Scaling spend and expecting flat CAC almost never works on its own. To hold ₹500, the incremental ₹1,00,000 has to produce its own 200 riders — which, combined with the base 200, means 400 total riders. At the current 20% conversion rate, that actually needs ~2,000 installs, not 1,500.

I'm treating 1,500 / 3,000 as the stated target — but the gap above is real, and the channel mix below is built to close as much of it as possible.

Test Order

  • First-ride offer strength — this moves the actual bottleneck (conversion rate), not just install volume.
  • Ad angle: "zero-cancellation" vs. "fixed price/no surge" — unclear which pain point hits harder yet.
  • Referral credit size (₹100 vs ₹150) — cheap channel, outsized sensitivity to incentive.

Tracking Plan

  • GA4 funnel: install → signup → first_ride → subscription_purchased.
  • UTM on every paid link and WhatsApp broadcast.
  • Unique referral codes per channel — attributes WhatsApp/referral bookings GA4 can't see.
  • One shared Google Sheet for weekly CAC-by-channel — maintainable in an hour a week, no data team required.

Risks

  • CAC creep as paid spend scales — auction costs rise with volume.
  • Referral cannibalization inflates the channel's apparent efficiency.
  • Bookings can miss even if installs hit target, since bookings also depend on existing riders' ride frequency.
  • Corporate-park hyperlocal channels saturate within 2–3 weeks.
  • WhatsApp API opt-in/rate limits cap how far referral broadcasts can scale.

If Week 2 Is Off-Target

  • Hard rule: any channel spending ≥₹15,000 with CAC >20% over ₹500 after week 1 gets paused immediately — no waiting for month-end.
  • CAC high, bookings fine → tighten targeting radius before cutting spend.
  • Installs fine, CAC high → shift budget to referral/hyperlocal even if it slows the install number.
  • Conversion rate hasn't moved → raise the first-ride incentive immediately — this is the actual constraint, not install volume.
